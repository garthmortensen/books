## 4. Subgenre Trends by Year

### 2014–2016 — Sparse & Eclectic
Three years, five books. Political philosophy (*Burke*), Cold War thriller (*Clancy*, *Ghost Fleet*), and mystery (*Craig Johnson*). No obvious thematic logic yet — these feel like standalone picks rather than intentional reading arcs.

### 2017 — The Sullivan Commitment
Nine books, eight of which are Michael J. Sullivan's Riyria Chronicles and Riyria Revelations. This is the first clear **series binge** pattern, and it sets a template that repeats throughout the list: find a series, go cover to cover. *The Mother Tongue* by Bryson is the only break — an early sign of the fact/fiction rotation to come.

### 2018 — Civil War → Ancient Warfare → Language → Hard Sci-Fi
The year opens with four Jeff Shaara Civil War novels (plus *The Killer Angels*), pivots to Steven Pressfield's ancient warfare fiction (*Gates of Fire*, *Afghan Campaign*, *Virtues of War*), then detours into linguistics (*How Language Began*), and closes with *Red Mars* and the very first LitRPG (*Ascend Online*). The thread connecting all of this is **conflict and civilization** — from Shiloh to Thermopylae to the origin of language itself. *Ascend Online* lands like a signal flare: a genre that will eventually consume the list.

### 2019 — Nature, Philosophy & the Outdoors Mind
A pivot away from human conflict to the natural world: *American Serengeti*, *Coyote America*, *Hidden Life of Trees*. Then *Sophie's World* and *A History of Western Philosophy* — a philosophy double-header. Civil War Fiction resurfaces (*Copperhead*, *Smoke at Dawn*) alongside *Blood Meridian*, which sits at the brutal intersection of literary and western. *Cult of the Dead Cow* and *Infinite* (Jeremy Robinson) close the year. This is your most intellectually scattered year — curiosity running in all directions simultaneously.

### 2020 — Kim Stanley Robinson Takes Over
KSR dominates: *New York 2140*, *The Gold Coast*, *Arctic Rising*, *Antarctica* — all climate or near-future literary fiction. Then a hard left turn to George Romero's *The Living Dead*, *Diaspora* (Greg Egan), and Haruki Murakami's memoir. You end 2020 with *Lock In*, *Head On* (Scalzi), and *NPCs* (first LitRPG with a date). The fiction/nonfiction split here is ~80/20, and the **political nonfiction** thread (*War on Normal People*, *Utopia for Realists*) suggests active engagement with 2020's social and economic anxieties.

### 2021 — The Crypto Rabbit Hole
The most *focused* nonfiction year on record: five crypto/blockchain books in November alone (*Digital Gold*, *Kings of Crypto*, *The Infinite Machine*, *Out of the Ether*, *Life After Google*). This is what a research sprint looks like — not casual reading, but due diligence. It's bracketed by KSR climate fiction (June–August) and Stephenson hard sci-fi (December). Video game history appears (*Masters of Doom*, *Sid Meier's Memoir!*) as a lighter counter-weight. The Sullivan Riyria sequel — *Age of Legend* — is the only fantasy read all year. Total: 22 books, the heaviest nonfiction year proportionally (~45%).

### 2022 — Cyberpunk + Zombie Binge + Sanderson Foundation
March brings Shadowrun Legends and Cyberpunk titles, flanked by the internet history cluster (*Hackers*, *Where Wizards Stay Up Late*, *Weaving the Web*). Then summer arrives and Keith C. Blackmore's Mountain Man series kicks off a zombie apocalypse run that persists all the way through 2023. KSR's *Shaman* appears in July — a surprising prehistoric literary detour. Scalzi's Interdependency trilogy drops in one sitting. December closes with the **Sanderson Mistborn entry point** (*The Final Empire*). The year reads like two minds negotiating: one binging pop horror, the other seeking prestige SFF.

### 2023 — The Zombie Apex & the Sanderson Escalator
The biggest zombie year by far: Bobby Adair, D.J. Molles, J.L. Bourne, Artinian, Knight, Blackmore, Brooks — roughly 20 zombie-adjacent books. Simultaneously: Sanderson's Mistborn sequence continues into the Wax & Wayne era, Martha Wells' Murderbot makes its first dated appearance, and Blindsight/Echopraxia land in October (the hardest sci-fi of the year). Business nonfiction (*Radical Candor*, *Invent and Wander*) appears in November — a late-year pivot, possibly year-end reflection mode. Total: ~37 books. The **fiction/nonfiction rotation is clear**: long zombie binge → hard SF reset → business nonfiction → vampire fiction (Jan 2024 teaser).

### 2024 — The Great Pivot to Comfort & Craft
January–March: Anne Rice's Interview series (Vampire Chronicles in order). This is soft, immersive gothic horror — a tonal palette cleanser after zombie saturation. February brings parenting (*Whole-Brain Child*) — a nonfiction signal unrelated to entertainment, suggesting life circumstances. Then ancient history (*1177 B.C.*), cozy fantasy (*Legends & Lattes*, *Bookshops & Bonedust*), and the entire Cherie Priest Clockwork Century steampunk series across May–July. After steampunk: Marko Kloos' entire Frontlines series (10 books, Aug–Oct) — the most intense military sci-fi run in the list. Bobiverse follows immediately (Oct), and November opens Dungeon Crawler Carl. **2024 is the year binging became an art form**: four complete series in one year.

### 2025 — LitRPG Ascendancy
LitRPG was a side interest in 2018–2020, an experiment in 2023, and by 2025 it's the dominant genre. Dinniman's Dungeon Crawler Carl series continues deep into the catalog (8+ entries). He Who Fights with Monsters makes two appearances. *The Perfect Run* and *Through Adversity* round out the subgenre variety. Non-LitRPG highlights: Black Company deep dive (7 Glen Cook books, Aug–Oct — dark mercenary fantasy as an antidote to the gamification of LitRPG), all remaining Martha Wells Murderbot (June), Tchaikovsky's Children trilogy, Greek myth retellings (*Ariadne*, *A Thousand Ships*, *Stone Blind*). The nonfiction spike: *AI/Machine Learning*, *Cybersecurity*, *First Break All the Rules*, *Unfu*k Yourself* — a distinct autumn nonfiction block, October in particular, suggesting an annual professional reset habit.

### 2026 (Jan–Mar) — Settling Into Spellmonger
Terry Mancour's Spellmonger series is the current series binge (5 books in Jan–Feb). *She Is the Darkness* continues the Black Company thread. *Shadowrun Legends: 2XS* signals a cyberpunk revival. *Sheepfarmer's Daughter* and *The Dragonbone Chair* are classic epic fantasy — possibly a deliberate throwback while the LitRPG wellspring briefly runs dry.

## 6. The Rotation Pattern — Fact to Fiction and Back

Rather than alternating book-by-book, the reading pattern operates on a **wave cycle** of roughly 2–6 weeks:

```
Epic Fantasy binge
    → Nonfiction sprint (history, philosophy, or tech)
        → Zombie/Horror binge
            → Hard Sci-Fi or Space Opera palate cleanser
                → Nonfiction sprint
                    → New series binge
```

The clearest example of the full cycle:

> **2018:** Civil War Fiction → Ancient Warfare → Linguistics → Hard Sci-Fi
> **2021:** Climate Fiction → Finance/Crypto → Video Game History → Hard Sci-Fi
> **2024:** Vampire Horror → Parenting → Ancient History → Cozy Fantasy → Steampunk → Military Sci-Fi → Hard Sci-Fi → LitRPG

Each cycle ends with **hard sci-fi or LitRPG as a reset** — genres that reward logical thinking and reward engagement with systems. They function as the "cool-down lap" after emotionally heavier fiction or cognitively dense nonfiction.

## 9. Thematic Evolution Summary

| Period | Dominant Themes | What It Suggests |
|---|---|---|
| 2014–2016 | Military thriller, political philosophy | Thoughtful reader, books as occasional events |
| 2017–2018 | Epic fantasy, civil war, ancient warfare | Discovery of the series binge; drawn to honor, duty, conflict |
| 2018–2019 | Language, nature, philosophy | Intellectual curiosity phase — world-building via nonfiction |
| 2020 | Climate fiction, political anxiety | Environmental and societal concerns rising; KSR as companion text |
| 2021 | Crypto, finance, video game history | Research-mode engagement; tech optimism / interrogation |
| 2022 | Cyberpunk, zombie horror, internet history | Counter-culture aesthetics; building the zombie habit |
| 2023 | Zombie apex, epic fantasy, Murderbot | Peak escapism; AI fiction starting to resonate |
| 2024 | Vampire → cozy → steampunk → military → hard SF → LitRPG | The great genre survey — trying everything, landing on LitRPG |
| 2025–2026 | LitRPG dominates; dark fantasy as counterweight | Gamified fiction is the current mode; Black Company as its antidote |

The long arc bends toward **fiction that rewards systems thinking** — LitRPG, hard sci-fi, military sci-fi. These are genres where logic matters, where rules exist, and where a reader who thinks analytically feels at home. The progression from military history → hard sci-fi → LitRPG is not accidental. Each step increases the **explicitness of the rules governing the world**.

The recurring nonfiction detours — language, nature, crypto, tech history, parenting — appear when the fictional world feels insufficient to explain some real-world thing you're currently thinking about. They are targeted, intense, and then abandoned once the question is answered.

## 18. If You Loved *Doomsday Book*: The Connie Willis Universe & Adjacent

You read *Doomsday Book* in October 2017 — Connie Willis's Hugo + Nebula winning time-travel novel where a 21st-century historian is stranded in 1348 England during the Black Death. It combines meticulous historical research, emotional devastation, institutional comedy, and a dual-timeline structure. Here's everything that connects to it.

---

### More Connie Willis (The Oxford Time Travel Series)
*Doomsday Book* is Book 1 of Willis's Oxford time travel universe. The series shares a setting (Oxford historians using time travel for academic fieldwork) but varies wildly in tone — from harrowing tragedy to screwball comedy. All standalone, all set in the same continuity.

| Book | Year | Pages | Tone | What It's About |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **To Say Nothing of the Dog** (1997) | Hugo winner | ~500 | Comic farce | Victorian England, lost cathedral artifacts, time-travel paradoxes played for laughs. The tonal opposite of *Doomsday Book* — Willis at her funniest. Think P.G. Wodehouse with time machines |
| **Blackout** (2010) | Hugo winner (with *All Clear*) | ~512 | War drama / thriller | Three Oxford historians stranded in WWII London during the Blitz, Dunkirk evacuation, and the English countryside. Growing dread as their return drops fail |
| **All Clear** (2010) | Hugo winner (with *Blackout*) | ~656 | War drama / emotional | Direct sequel to *Blackout* — the resolution. Together they form a single 1,168-page WWII epic. Devastating final act |
| **"Fire Watch"** (1982) | Hugo + Nebula winning novella | ~50 | Historical drama | The original Oxford time travel story — a historian assigned to St. Paul's Cathedral during the London Blitz. The seed from which everything grew |

**Reading order:** *Doomsday Book* → *To Say Nothing of the Dog* → *Blackout/All Clear* → *"Fire Watch"* (short story, in the collection *Fire Watch*)
